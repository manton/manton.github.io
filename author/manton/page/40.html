No perfect iPhone size

Some people bought the iPhone 6 and then went back to the 5S. Some people bought the iPhone 6 Plus and then tried the 6. Some worked their way up to the 6 Plus after adapting to the 6. Some never upgraded to the 6 or 6 Plus because both are too big.

Marco‚Äôs post is a good formal summary of a few write-ups I‚Äôve read this week:

‚ÄúHaving used an iPhone 6 full-time from its launch until these 6 Plus experiments over the last few weeks, I can confidently say that neither phone is extremely well-designed. Both have nontrivial and completely avoidable flaws. But the 6 Plus has bigger advantages over the other phones, while the 6 seems to sit in a mediocre middle ground.‚Äù

The lesson from all these switches couldn’t be more clear: there’s no longer one perfect iPhone for everyone. What works great for one person might be terrible for someone else. I personally love the 5C design ‚Äî the size of the screen, the way the plastic feels in my hand, flipping or spinning it on my fingers without worry that it‚Äôll slip, using it without a case, adding a little color to my life ‚Äî but many people never even tried it because it contains underpowered hardware compared to the latest models.

Apple would be crazy to discontinue any size. I’m more convinced than ever that we’ll see a 4-inch 6C alongside a new 6S and 6S Plus later this year. They won’t have identical specs, and that’s okay. I’ll happily pick the 4-inch model even if its camera is a year behind the cutting edge. The iPhone market is so ginormous now that I know there are millions of people who feel the same way.

If the post is read by so few people, some might argue that it can‚Äôt be as relevant to a larger conversation.

This doesn‚Äôt bum me out, though. It inspires me. It reminds me that I believe in something ambitious that has to be built in layers, starting small ‚Äî¬†a more open microblog platform that other apps can hang on to, encouraging new writing that will last.

Dave Winer calls this process of building successful platforms a coral reef. I think it‚Äôs a forest. How to start a microblog

All my short, microblog-style posts go to my own weblog first, and also get cross-posted manually to App.net. You can view them in a special category and RSS feed.

I think owning your own posts on the internet ‚Äî even if they seem unimportant and fleeting ‚Äî is a valuable contribution to the health of the open web. By loosely following some simple conventions, we can build stuff that goes beyond what purely centralized web apps like Twitter are capable of.

Getting started is easy. I recommend one of 3 approaches right now if you want to play in this emerging ecosystem:

  • Tumblr. Microblog posts don‚Äôt need titles, and Tumblr has never cared much for titles itself. Some of Tumblr‚Äôs post types fit the style of a microblog very well. They also provide custom domain mapping, so that if you want to move your site later you can do it without breaking links.
  • Radio3. The latest version of Dave Winer‚Äôs tool can cross-post to Twitter. The setup couldn‚Äôt be easier, and because it has it‚Äôs own RSS feed, it will be easy to plug into future apps or get your data out.
  • WordPress. I use the self-hosted version of this. Just give microblog posts the ‚Äústatus‚Äù post type, which many WordPress themes can render with a tweet-like style. If you also put these posts in a special category, you can provide RSS feeds just for certain post types, or filter them out of your main feed.

Since last year I‚Äôve been working on something new that is all about microblogging. I hope that it will encourage many more microblogs, but there‚Äôs no reason to wait until then. You can start a microblog today with one of the above apps (or dozens of other blogging solutions), and more fully control your own presence on the web.
